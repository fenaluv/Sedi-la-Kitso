Fixing a Leaking Tap — A Simple, Lasting Repair

Published: 2022-04-12 — By Fena

A leaking tap is small, noisy and wastes water — and it’s often a quick job you can handle. I’m Fena — I’ll walk you step-by-step so you finish the repair with confidence.

Why fix it now?

Even a small drip can waste many litres of water each day. Fixing a leaking tap saves water, lowers bills, and keeps your fixtures lasting longer. This guide is for common compression and cartridge taps found in many homes.

Tools & parts

  • Adjustable wrench and pliers
  • Replacement washers or a new cartridge (match the tap model)
  • Flathead screwdriver, cloth, bucket
  • Plumber’s grease and plumber’s tape (for threaded joints)

Step-by-step

  1. Turn off the water: Close the isolated valve to the tap or the house stopcock. Open the tap to drain any remaining water.
  2. Protect the drain: Plug the sink or use a cloth so small parts don’t fall into the drain.
  3. Remove the handle: Use a screwdriver or allen key to access the cartridge or valve beneath. Keep screws in a small bowl.
  4. Inspect the parts: For compression taps, a worn washer is often the cause. For mixer taps, the cartridge might be the worn part. Compare old parts to new ones to be sure.
  5. Replace and reassemble: Use the correct size washer or cartridge. Lightly grease moving seals and reassemble carefully. Apply plumber’s tape to any threaded connections, but don’t overuse it.
  6. Test: Turn water on slowly, check for leaks, and tighten gently if necessary. Don’t overtighten — it can crack seats or ceramic spindles.
Pro tip: Take a picture of the disassembled tap before you remove parts. It helps reassembly and shows the part type when buying replacements.

Common problems and quick fixes

If the tap still leaks after a new washer, the valve seat might be corroded or cracked — a worn valve seat often needs a professional touch or a full replacement of the tap body.

Safety and water-saving note

Always work with the water off. For older houses, watch for brittle seals and corroded nuts. Fixing leaks helps conserve water in our communities — a small act with big impact.

Smart Leak Detection for Small Homes — Practical Setup on a Budget

Published: 2024-10-11 — By Fena

Smart leak detection isn’t only for big houses. In 2024 there are affordable devices and simple practices to catch leaks early and reuse greywater safely for garden irrigation. This post shows practical, budget-friendly steps you can use today.

Why smart leak detection matters

Leaks often go unseen until damage shows. A small, early-detected leak can save thousands in repair costs. New devices can alert you on your phone, shut water at the house valve, or give local alarms — many below R1000–R2000 now make sense for small homes.

What kit do you need?

  • Water sensor pads (Wi-Fi or Bluetooth models) — placed under appliances and near drains
  • A smart shutoff valve (optional) — installs on the main water line for automatic closure
  • Simple greywater diverter kit for garden reuse (outlet and filters)
  • Phone with the product app (or a cheap Wi-Fi hub) for notifications

Step-by-step: Basic setup

  1. Place sensors: Under the washing machine, dishwasher, near water heaters, and under bathroom cabinets. Sensors with battery backup keep working during outages.
  2. Link to phone: Use the device app and test notifications. Configure the sensor sensitivity so it doesn’t false alarm from small spills.
  3. Install a diverter for greywater: Use an inline filter and a safe diversion path to a garden tank. Greywater from showers and basins can be reused for garden watering — avoid kitchen sink water without treatment.
  4. Consider a smart shutoff: For homes with valuables, a small shutoff valve that triggers on sensor alerts adds protection — consider professional install for the valve itself.
Pro tip: Start with one sensor near the washing machine — it’s the most common source of leaks. Learn the device’s false alarm behaviour before you add more sensors.

Maintenance & community benefit

Test sensors monthly and check batteries. If you run a local training session, show neighbours how a sensor works — small community adoption cuts damage and keeps local costs lower when technicians share tools and knowledge.

Basic Home Wiring — Safety, Colors and First Checks

Published: 2022-05-22 — By Fena

Home wiring looks scary until you learn the rules. This guide covers safety, conductor colors, how to isolate a circuit, and simple tests with a multimeter. Keep it safe — and when in doubt, call a licensed electrician.

Core safety rules

  1. Switch off the circuit at the consumer unit (breaker panel) and lock it out if possible.
  2. Use an RCD (residual current device) for extra protection when testing circuits.
  3. Never touch live wires with bare hands — use insulated tools and gloves.

Common wire colors (local guidance)

Brown (live), Blue (neutral), Green/Yellow (earth). Older systems may differ — check labeling.

Preparing Home Wiring for Solar-Ready Systems (Practical Steps)

Published: 2024-09-02 — By Fena

As solar gets affordable, homes need simple planning. This 2024 guide explains how to prepare your wiring and consumer unit for a later solar battery or inverter hookup — practical steps anyone can follow or hand to a technician.

What 'solar-ready' means

It means the home has a clear, labeled circuit for the inverter, properly rated cables, space in the consumer unit, and appropriately placed earthing and isolation points.

Checklist

  • Ensure the consumer unit has spare ways or space for an inverter isolator.
  • Run suitable cable (copper, rated for current) from the inverter location to the consumer unit — avoid long thin runs that cause voltage drop.
  • Label the circuits and leave documentation for the installer.
Pro tip: If a homeowner plans solar in the next 2–3 years, adding a properly rated conduit now saves big later.
